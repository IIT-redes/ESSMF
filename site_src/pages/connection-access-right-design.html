Access-right design

What capacity right is granted, and under which network conditions?

Understand the dimensionComment on this dimension

Clear definition

What this dimension means

Access-right design specifies whether the user receives firm, partially firm, interruptible, conditional, dynamic, temporary, or long-term capacity. It defines the legal and operational meaning of being connected.

The access right is the foundation of the agreement. If it is vague, neither the DSO nor the user can value the connection, finance the project, plan operation, or determine whether curtailment is compliant.

Design choices

Choices that must be made explicitly

A reviewer should be able to identify which option is selected, why it is selected, who decides it, and how it is implemented.

Firmness

Firm, non-firm, partially firm, conditional, dynamic, or emergency-only limitation.

Direction

Import, export, or both, with potentially different limits.

Capacity envelope

Fixed limit, scheduled profile, seasonal limit, or dynamic operating envelope.

Duration

Temporary bridge, fixed-term contract, long-term arrangement, or periodic renewal.

Practical example

How the dimension appears in practice

A solar-plus-storage project receives 10 MW firm export and up to 20 MW when local capacity is available. The agreement must define how the additional 10 MW is calculated, communicated, and treated when the network changes.

Review principle

The mechanism is credible only when the selected design can be explained in plain language and linked to a verifiable network, regulatory, commercial, or customer outcome.

Stakeholder implications

The same design choice creates different responsibilities and risks

DSO

Define an access envelope that is secure, measurable, and operationally manageable.

Regulator

Ensure comparable users receive transparent and non-discriminatory offers.

Developer or aggregator

Model revenue and dispatch under the granted access right.

Customer or FSP

Understand the practical difference between firm and flexible capacity.

Questions for reviewers

What remains open?

  • What exactly is guaranteed?
  • Which network states change the right?
  • Is the flexible option genuinely voluntary?
  • Can the right be transferred with the asset?
  • When and how can it become firm?

Evidence expected

What should support the decision?

  • Capacity and contingency studies
  • Published eligibility methodology
  • Comparison with a firm-connection offer
  • Historical availability of flexible capacity
  • Contract bankability assessment
