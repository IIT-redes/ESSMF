Temporal and locational resolution

How precisely should the tariff identify when and where network use is costly?

Understand the dimensionComment on this dimension

Clear definition

What this dimension means

Resolution describes the level of detail in the tariff signal. Temporal resolution may range from annual or seasonal charges to hourly or dynamic signals. Locational resolution may range from a national tariff to zones, feeders, substations, or connection points.

Greater granularity can improve cost-reflectivity, but it also increases complexity, volatility, data requirements, and the risk that customers cannot understand or act on the signal.

Design choices

Choices that must be made explicitly

A reviewer should be able to identify which option is selected, why it is selected, who decides it, and how it is implemented.

Temporal structure

Annual, seasonal, peak/off-peak, hourly, critical-peak, or dynamic.

Locational structure

System-wide, regional, zonal, feeder-level, substation-level, or connection-point.

Notice period

Long-term published parameters, day-ahead notice, intraday update, or near-real-time signal.

Stability rule

How frequently parameters may change and how customers are protected from abrupt redesign.

Practical example

How the dimension appears in practice

A substation is constrained only on a few summer afternoons. A broad annual charge spreads the signal across all hours and users. A targeted critical-peak period can be more effective, but customers need reliable advance notice and the DSO needs a transparent trigger for declaring the period.

Review principle

The mechanism is credible only when the selected design can be explained in plain language and linked to a verifiable network, regulatory, commercial, or customer outcome.

Stakeholder implications

The same design choice creates different responsibilities and risks

DSO

Choose sufficient granularity to address the constraint without creating an operational control tariff.

Regulator

Balance precision with predictability, simplicity, and equal treatment.

Aggregator or retailer

Forecast the signal and integrate it into portfolio optimization.

Customer or FSP

Receive a signal early enough to act without unacceptable disruption.

Questions for reviewers

What remains open?

  • What is the minimum resolution needed to address the network problem?
  • Does the metering infrastructure support that resolution?
  • How much advance notice is necessary?
  • Could neighbouring users face materially different charges without clear justification?
  • How should parameters evolve as the network changes?

Evidence expected

What should support the decision?

  • Constraint duration and location analysis
  • Metering and communication capability
  • Customer response lead-time studies
  • Forecast-error assessment
  • Complexity and comprehension testing
