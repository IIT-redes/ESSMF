What is the proposal?

A market framework is not a diagram. It is a grammar for change.

New flexibility markets are emerging around congestion management, voltage control, balancing, local system services, and distributed resource activation. Without a common structure, each market can become an isolated vocabulary. The Theoretical Market Framework proposes a compact way to describe architecture, coordination, clearing, operation, and grid representation.

Its purpose is not to force every country or demonstrator into the same design. It is to make design choices explicit, comparable, and discussable.

Why does it matter?

Flexibility is a coordination problem before it becomes a market product.

Distribution-connected resources can support both local and system-wide needs. The same resource may be relevant for a DSO constraint, a TSO balancing need, a local congestion product, or an intraday position.

The critical design question is therefore not only what is the product, but how interacting markets avoid conflict and create value.

The structure of exchange

Market architecture

Market architecture defines what is traded, where it is traded, which actors participate, and how individual sub-markets form one coordinated design space.

  • Number, type, and timing of sub-markets.
  • Services and products procured for system operation.
  • Locational scope, voltage level, and procurement area.
  • Roles of TSOs, DSOs, market operators, aggregators, flexibility providers, and platform operators.

The logic of interaction

Sub-market coordination

Sub-market coordination defines how flexibility is allocated when several markets, buyers, or system needs compete for the same distributed resources.

  • Allocation principles for flexibility across buyers and needs.
  • Coordination timing before, during, or after clearing.
  • Bid forwarding between related markets.
  • Avoiding double use, conflicting activation, and fragmented decisions.

The clearing intelligence

Market optimization

Market optimization defines how bids, constraints, objectives, and sub-market interactions are transformed into a clearing result.

  • Optimization methodology and mathematical representation.
  • Separate, sequential, centralized, or simultaneous clearing.
  • Objective functions such as cost, welfare, security, or feasibility.
  • Use of OPF, simplified linear methods, sensitivities, or market rules.

The procedure of trust

Market operation

Market operation defines the practical rules that make the market usable: remuneration, clearing type, procurement frequency, bid properties, and market phases.

  • Remuneration scheme and product-attribute remuneration.
  • Market-clearing type and settlement logic.
  • Procurement frequency: long-term, day-ahead, intraday, or near-real-time.
  • Prequalification, baselining, activation, validation, and settlement.

The physics inside the market

Network representation

Network representation defines how physical constraints, topology, voltage, congestion, and location are included in the market design.

  • Representation of network constraints in or around clearing.
  • Timing of grid validation and constraint inclusion.
  • Use of zones, nodes, feeders, voltage levels, or virtual buses.
  • Sensitivity-based approximations for scalable local flexibility markets.
