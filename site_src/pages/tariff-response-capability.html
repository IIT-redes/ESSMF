Response capability

Can the exposed customers change consumption or injection in the required way?

Understand the dimensionComment on this dimension

Clear definition

What this dimension means

Response capability links the tariff signal to realistic customer action. It includes flexible demand, smart EV charging, storage, generation control, thermal inertia, industrial scheduling, and automated energy-management systems.

A theoretically efficient tariff is ineffective if customers lack controllable assets, information, automation, or authority over their consumption. Response capability must therefore be assessed before assigning network cost responsibility.

Design choices

Choices that must be made explicitly

A reviewer should be able to identify which option is selected, why it is selected, who decides it, and how it is implemented.

Eligible resources

Demand, EVs, heat pumps, storage, distributed generation, and industrial processes.

Control method

Manual action, automated local control, retailer service, or aggregator dispatch.

Response limit

Power, duration, energy, comfort, mobility, production, and rebound constraints.

Participation model

Default exposure, optional tariff, protected class, or delegated response service.

Practical example

How the dimension appears in practice

Two customers have the same peak demand, but one owns a battery and automated EV charger while the other is a tenant with no control over heating. Exposing both to the same dynamic capacity signal may be formally equal but practically unequal.

Review principle

The mechanism is credible only when the selected design can be explained in plain language and linked to a verifiable network, regulatory, commercial, or customer outcome.

Stakeholder implications

The same design choice creates different responsibilities and risks

DSO

Estimate dependable response rather than assuming elasticity.

Regulator

Prevent customers without response capability from bearing disproportionate risk.

Aggregator or retailer

Offer automation and risk-management services while disclosing value sharing.

Customer or FSP

Decide whether the benefit justifies equipment, inconvenience, or delegated control.

Questions for reviewers

What remains open?

  • Which customer segments have meaningful flexibility?
  • How reliable is automated response?
  • What rebound follows the response period?
  • Should non-flexible customers receive alternative treatment?
  • How is delegated control governed and monitored?

Evidence expected

What should support the decision?

  • Load and asset segmentation
  • Elasticity and automation trials
  • Comfort and service-quality impacts
  • Rebound and persistence measurements
  • Cost and payback of enabling technology
