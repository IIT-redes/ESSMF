Coordination with other signals

How should tariffs coexist with retail prices, balancing, local markets, and connection conditions?

Understand the dimensionComment on this dimension

Clear definition

What this dimension means

Coordination defines how the tariff interacts with other economic and operational signals that may reach the same asset. These include wholesale prices, retail contracts, balancing activation, local flexibility markets, connection agreements, and direct DSO control.

Signals can complement one another, but they can also conflict, contaminate baselines, create double payment, or lead an asset to respond in a direction that is efficient for one market and harmful for the local network.

Design choices

Choices that must be made explicitly

A reviewer should be able to identify which option is selected, why it is selected, who decides it, and how it is implemented.

Priority rule

Which instruction or incentive takes precedence under conflicting conditions.

Baseline rule

How routine tariff response is separated from explicitly remunerated activation.

Stacking rule

When the same asset may combine revenue streams and when this is prohibited.

Information exchange

What DSOs, retailers, aggregators, and market operators must share.

Practical example

How the dimension appears in practice

An EV fleet faces a low wholesale price overnight but a local network peak in the same period. The retailer encourages charging while the network tariff discourages it. Without a clear priority and customer proposition, the final behaviour is uncertain.

Review principle

The mechanism is credible only when the selected design can be explained in plain language and linked to a verifiable network, regulatory, commercial, or customer outcome.

Stakeholder implications

The same design choice creates different responsibilities and risks

DSO

Protect network security without unnecessarily blocking market participation.

Regulator

Prevent double recovery, double reward, and discriminatory priority rules.

Aggregator or retailer

Optimize a portfolio with predictable signal hierarchy and data.

Customer or FSP

Avoid contradictory instructions and unclear liability.

Questions for reviewers

What remains open?

  • What happens when wholesale and network signals point in opposite directions?
  • How is tariff response reflected in local-market baselines?
  • Can an asset receive a tariff benefit and an explicit payment for the same action?
  • Which actor communicates the final customer instruction?
  • What data exchange is necessary and proportionate?

Evidence expected

What should support the decision?

  • Co-optimization and conflict simulations
  • Baseline sensitivity studies
  • Revenue-stacking analysis
  • Operational event logs
  • Customer and aggregator workflow testing
