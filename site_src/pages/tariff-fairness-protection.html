Fairness and protection

How should costs, benefits, risks, and opportunities be distributed?

Understand the dimensionComment on this dimension

Clear definition

What this dimension means

Fairness and protection address whether the tariff allocates costs according to network causation while protecting customers who cannot respond, cannot invest in automation, or face essential service needs.

Average efficiency can hide severe distributional effects. A tariff may reduce total network cost while increasing bills for vulnerable, rural, low-income, tenant, or inflexible customers.

Design choices

Choices that must be made explicitly

A reviewer should be able to identify which option is selected, why it is selected, who decides it, and how it is implemented.

Fairness principle

Cost causation, beneficiary pays, ability to respond, social protection, or a balanced combination.

Protection instrument

Bill cap, opt-out, social tariff, transition period, exemption, or targeted support.

Legacy treatment

How existing customers and assets transition to a new charging methodology.

Transparency

How bill impacts, assumptions, and cross-subsidies are communicated.

Practical example

How the dimension appears in practice

A capacity-based tariff rewards households that can flatten demand with a battery but may increase the bill of a low-income household with electric heating and no capital for automation. The regulator must decide whether the cost signal is justified and which protection is appropriate.

Review principle

The mechanism is credible only when the selected design can be explained in plain language and linked to a verifiable network, regulatory, commercial, or customer outcome.

Stakeholder implications

The same design choice creates different responsibilities and risks

DSO

Explain cost causation and avoid hidden cross-subsidies between network levels.

Regulator

Assess affordability, inclusion, and proportionality alongside efficiency.

Aggregator or retailer

Design offers that do not conceal risk or capture most of the benefit.

Customer or FSP

Receive understandable information, practical choices, and effective remedies.

Questions for reviewers

What remains open?

  • Who gains and who loses under the design?
  • Can affected customers alter the relevant behaviour?
  • Which essential uses need protection?
  • How long should transitional arrangements last?
  • How should intermediaries disclose savings and fees?

Evidence expected

What should support the decision?

  • Bill-impact distributions
  • Income and vulnerability analysis
  • Technology-ownership and tenancy data
  • Customer comprehension studies
  • Assessment of protection alternatives

Feedback on fairness and protection
